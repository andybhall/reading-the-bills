\begin{abstract}
\noindent The models political science uses to measure Congress
predict rollcall votes without reading the bills being voted on. We
construct an open benchmark of 10.6 million individual voting
decisions (101st--119th Congresses) and evaluate models whose
language-model encoders take the text of each measure as input.
Because floor agendas are curated, a bill's pre-vote text encodes the
political situation its arrival creates---a must-pass fiscal deal, a
consensus suspension item, a program-building authorization---and
reading it amounts to measuring agenda politics, not only bill
content. Three results follow. First, the approach outperforms
existing ones: a flexible vote-only spatial model improves on
DW-NOMINATE on identical held-out votes precisely for the members
one-dimensional scaling is known to mis-measure (the share of Ron
Paul's held-out votes classified correctly rises from \paulCCnom\% to
\paulCCours\%), and on
strictly future votes a frozen, hash-pinned text-reading artifact
sustains \champForecastAcc\% accuracy (log loss \champForecastLL{}
for the ensemble, \incForecastLL{} under fully leakage-clean inputs),
beyond both classical spatial models and the earlier text-based
literature. Second, text lets the model anticipate majority-party
defections---including canonical protest episodes---before the vote:
across \protRC{} held-out majority-side rollcalls, reading the bill
raises within-vote defection detection from \protAUCnotext{} to
\protAUCtext{} AUC, with redaction ablations and counterfactual text
edits locating the signal in bill-specific content and procedural
posture. Third, text locates bills on the realized agenda before
anyone votes: on \cutTestN{} held-out future rollcalls, text plus
pre-vote metadata predicts realized rollcall cutpoints at
$r = \cutComboR$ and coalition direction at \dirCombo\%. The
framework's predicted boundaries---a Senate where text adds little
and procedural histories that fail at majority transitions---are
measured and reported. We release the benchmark, the models, an
interactive forecaster, and a pre-registered ledger of every
experiment, positive and negative.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Nearly everything political science measures about Congress---ideal
points, party unity, issue positions---derives from models of
rollcall votes that treat the bill under consideration as an indexed
event with estimable parameters rather than as a text with content
\citep{poole1997congress, clinton2004statistical}. A literature at
the intersection of machine learning and legislative studies sought
to change this with the text tools of its era
\citep{gerrish2011predicting, kraft2016embedding}, and the
measurement literature has since pressed on the classical model from
several directions: unpredictable voters
\citep{lauderdale2010unpredictable}, issue-specific dimensions
\citep{lauderdale2014scaling, crespin2010dimensions}, estimators for
massive vote matrices \citep{imai2016fast}, machine learning as a
measurement strategy \citep{bonica2018inferring}, and, most recently,
purpose-built models for the members whom one-dimensional scaling
visibly mis-measures---extremists who vote with the opposite pole
\citep{duckmayr2023ends} and majority members who cast protest votes
against bills they arguably prefer \citep{fowler2026protest}. Modern
language models read legislative text far better than anything
available to the earlier program. This paper asks what that capacity
contributes to the measurement of Congress. Our answer has a frame
and three results. The frame (Section~\ref{sec:theory}): because
floor agendas are curated by majority leadership
\citep{cox2005setting}, a bill's pre-vote text encodes the political
situation its arrival creates, so reading bills measures agenda
politics---which bills produce which coalitions---as much as bill
content; the constructs of member preference, institutional role, and
agenda-conditional coalition must therefore be kept apart, and each
empirical section is assigned to one of them.

First, the approach outperforms existing ones
(Section~\ref{sec:outperform}), and the section keeps its two
contributions explicitly apart. One is a vote-only result: on an open
benchmark of 10.6 million voting decisions spanning the 101st through
119th Congresses, in the psychometric completion setting with no text
anywhere in the model, an expressive spatial model classifies
held-out votes more accurately than frozen DW-NOMINATE for
\memberCCshare\% of members on the field's canonical statistic, with
the improvements concentrated in precisely the cases that motivated a
decade of bespoke scaling models. The other is the text result: in genuine
forecasting on the text-era congresses (108th--119th), a text-reading
ensemble attains \champForecastAcc\% accuracy on strictly future
votes (\champContestedAcc\% on contested votes), where the
2011--2016 text-based models, reimplemented and evaluated under
identical conditions, do not improve on a simple table of member
voting rates. A frozen artifact, hash-pinned before the votes it is
scored on were cast, verifies that these figures do not reflect
researcher iteration on the test set.

Second, reading the bill lets the model anticipate majority-party
defections before the vote (Section~\ref{sec:protest}). Defections
include the protest votes that are the scaling literature's newest
concern \citep{fowler2026protest}---treated there retrospectively, by
reinterpreting votes already cast---alongside sincere disagreement
and constituency pressure. We show that defections are predictable in
advance and locate the source of the predictability in the text.
Member history identifies who sits on a bill's losing flank; only the
bill's content indicates which bills put the flank in revolt. We
document the mechanism directly: in the encoder's embedding space, a
shutdown-averting continuing resolution sits in a neighborhood of
earlier must-pass fiscal packages that provoked the same right-flank
defections; a linear probe on the text alone predicts each holdout
rollcall's majority-defection share with no member-level information;
and redaction ablations and counterfactual edits show which parts of
the text do the work.

Third, text predicts where a bill will divide the chamber before
anyone votes (Section~\ref{sec:cutpoints}). The bill-location
literature estimates proposal and status-quo positions from realized
votes and cosponsorships \citep{peress2013estimating,
richman2011parties}; by construction it cannot locate a bill that has
not been voted on. We show that a bill's text can: on held-out future
rollcalls, text plus pre-vote metadata predicts realized cutpoint
locations at $r = \cutComboR$ and coalition direction at \dirCombo\%.
The division of labor among the inputs is itself informative:
institutional metadata indicates where a vote will cut, the acting
legislator's party supplies a default side, and the bill's content
indicates when the default is wrong. An interactive forecaster
operationalizes the result for hypothetical bills.

The remainder of what this exercise produced---evaluation-regime
audits, negative results with diagnosed mechanisms, the behavior of
member history across majority transitions---appears in the
appendices and repository. Section~\ref{sec:limits} reports the two
measured boundaries the framework predicts.

\section{What Bill Text Can Measure}
\label{sec:theory}

Because the empirical sections mix prediction and measurement, it is
worth being precise at the outset about what is being measured, and
what role bill text is hypothesized to play.

Three constructs must be kept apart. The first is \emph{member
preference}: the stable trait the ideal-point literature aims at,
identified from votes under the assumption that the agenda supplies
exogenous variation. The second is \emph{institutional role}: behavior
that attaches to a member's position in the chamber---above all,
majority-party support for the procedural motions that operate the
floor---and that changes when majority control changes, independent of
preference. The third is the \emph{agenda-conditional coalition}: the
particular alignment of members that a particular bill, arriving on
the floor through a particular procedure at a particular moment,
produces. Realized rollcall cutpoints, majority defections, and vote
forecasts are all facts about this third object. Classical scaling
treats the third construct as a nuisance through which to estimate
the first; this paper measures the third directly and asks what doing
so returns to the study of the other two.

The hypothesis about text follows from how bills reach the floor.
Floor agendas are curated by majority leadership
\citep{cox2005setting}: what receives a vote, under what procedure,
is itself a political choice. A bill's pre-vote text---its title,
its summary, the question under which it is called---therefore
encodes not only policy content but the \emph{situation} the
leadership has created: a must-pass fiscal package negotiated across
parties, a consensus item brought up under suspension, a
program-building authorization, a messaging resolution. Members'
responses to situations of a given kind recur. A model that reads the
text can recognize the situation before the vote and price the
coalition it typically produces; a model that sees only member
histories and indexed rollcalls cannot. On this account, text
prediction is measurement of \emph{agenda politics}---which bills
create which coalitions---as much as of bill ideology, and the
empirical sections should be read against that construct.

The account makes three commitments that the data can check, and
Sections~\ref{sec:outperform}--\ref{sec:limits} check all three.
First, text should improve forecasts most where the agenda is curated
by a majority party whose situations recur---the House---and least
where floor access is negotiated vote by vote and the marginal
information in the text is smaller---the Senate. Second, the
component of member behavior that is institutional role rather than
preference---procedural support---should fail to transfer across
majority transitions, and text should not rescue it. Third, where an
actor's identity defines the situation (amendments, where the
amender's party tells members most of what they need), adding the
actor's language should add little. All three patterns appear, and we
present them as content of the framework rather than as unexplained
boundaries. Conversely, the framework disciplines our claims: the
completion-regime result of Section~\ref{sec:outperform} involves no
text and is evidence about flexible vote-only measurement, not about
reading bills; and defection forecasting measures coalition behavior,
of which protest voting in the sense of \citet{fowler2026protest} is
one theoretically distinguished part.

\section{Benchmark, Models, and Definitions}
\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Task and data.} We predict individual voting decisions: given
everything observable before a rollcall, each member's probability of
voting yea, conditional on a recorded yea or nay. The panel comprises
every such decision in the 101st--119th Congresses from Voteview
\citep{lewis2026voteview}; bill-side information (titles, dated
summary versions, sponsors, amendment records) comes from government
BILLSTATUS files, which begin with the 108th Congress. All
forecasting results therefore use the text-era congresses
(108th--119th); earlier congresses enter only the no-text completion
fits. A benchmark card---composition by congress block and chamber,
bill-linkage and text-coverage shares, and holdout performance by
coverage cell---appears as Appendix Table~\ref{tab:benchcard}. Splits
are deterministic and content-hashed, the evaluation harness strips
labels before models see evaluation rows, and the feature-timing
audit appears in Appendix~\ref{app:audit}.

\textbf{Evaluation.} We evaluate under progressively stricter
information constraints: \emph{completion}, in which random votes are
held out and same-rollcall votes remain observable (the psychometric
setting in which ideal-point models are traditionally assessed);
\emph{temporal forecast}, in which each congress-chamber's final ten
percent of rollcalls by date is held out; and \emph{prospective}, in
which hash-pinned frozen artifacts are scored only on votes cast
after they were finalized, following the reusable-holdout logic of
\citet{dwork2015holdout}. Metrics are the field's standard ones:
correct classification, APRE, and geometric mean probability
\citep{carroll2013structure}, with log loss ($= -\log \mathrm{GMP}$)
as the primary scoring rule.

\textbf{Models.} The forecasting model is a logistic blend of
two-tower networks: member embeddings interact with rollcall
parameters that a deep head amortizes from pre-vote text (the vote
question, the vote description, and the latest bill summary dated
before the vote), anchored on member-by-question empirical rates and
calibrated on a temporal slice of the training data. Its no-text
counterpart---the identical architecture with all text inputs
deleted---serves as the counterfactual throughout. The classical
comparators are logistic ideal-point models, a frozen-DW-NOMINATE
model, and reimplementations of the 2011--2016 text-based models.
The full specification appears in Appendix~\ref{app:spec};
development followed a pre-registered experiment ledger
(Appendix~\ref{app:ledger}).

\textbf{Definitions.} The spatial fits estimate $P(\text{yea}) =
\sigma(a_v x_i + b_v + c_i)$ per chamber-congress (party-sign
initialized); a rollcall's \emph{average-member cutpoint} is
$-(b_v + \bar c)/a_v$, reported when $|a_v| \geq 0.35$ on the fitted
scale, with \emph{direction} $\mathrm{sign}(a_v)$. All derived
quantities are conditional on the realized rollcall agenda. The
identifying normalization: member positions are standardized within
chamber-congress (mean zero, unit variance) and oriented so that the
Republican mean exceeds the Democratic mean; cutpoints, the
$|a_v|$ screen, and directions are stated on that scale.

\section{The Approach Outperforms Existing Ones}
\label{sec:outperform}

The classical toolkit is assessed in two settings---completing a vote
matrix and forecasting future votes---and we take each in turn.

\subsection{Held-out fit against the canonical measure}

The completion regime involves no text: it asks only how well a
spatial model of votes fits held-out votes, and so isolates model
flexibility from bill content. In it, an eight-dimensional spatial
model reaches a geometric mean probability of \regimeAbestGMP{}
(\regimeAbestAcc\% correct classification) on held-out votes, against
\regimeAnominateGMP{} for frozen DW-NOMINATE positions with estimated
rollcall parameters; the full ladder of intermediate models appears
in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:regimeA}. Both models are fit on identical
training cells and scored on held-out cells that neither model's
parameters---including the rollcall parameters---were estimated on.
Figure~\ref{fig:membergmp} disaggregates the comparison to the member
level on the field's canonical statistic, the percent of held-out
votes correctly classified \citep{poole1997congress}. Two features of
the figure organize the result. For the typical member, DW-NOMINATE
is nearly unimprovable: the median member is classified correctly
\memberCCmedNom\% of the time under the classical model and
\memberCCmedOurs\% under ours, with the flexible model more accurate
for \memberCCshare\% of members (\memberGMPshare\% under geometric
mean probability, the proper-scoring variant, whose member-level
version tells the same story). The improvements instead concentrate
in the tail---and the tail is composed of exactly the members for
whom the past decade of scaling research built bespoke models. The
share of Ron Paul's held-out votes classified correctly rises from
\paulCCnom\% to \paulCCours\%; the other labeled members are
ends-against-the-middle voters and habitual defectors of the kind
modeled by \citet{duckmayr2023ends} and \citet{fowler2026protest},
including the centrist Democrats one-dimensional scaling handled
worst in the 1990s and 2000s (Penny, Condit, Murphy). One instructive
absence: today's left flank is \emph{not} in this tail. Frozen
DW-NOMINATE already classifies Ilhan Omar at \omarCCnom\% and Rashida
Tlaib at \tlaibCCnom\%, because members at the extreme of the scale
are easy to classify even when individual protest votes are missed;
their measurement story is about defection forecasting, and
Section~\ref{sec:protest} returns to them there.
Table~\ref{tab:hardcase} takes the two canonical hard cases vote by
vote: the held-out votes on which the flexible model most improves
are exactly the votes that made these members famous cases. For Paul
(\paulHeldoutN{} held-out votes), the largest improvements are nay
votes on defense authorizations and appropriations, an intelligence
authorization, the Electronic Surveillance Modernization Act, and an
Iran-focused resolution---votes the one-dimensional model prices
below two percent and the flexible model above ninety-five. For
Amash (\amashHeldoutN{}), they are rules resolutions, a commemorative
commission extension, an Israeli--Palestinian policy resolution, and
the FY2019 national defense authorization. Convergent validity is
preserved:
the model's first-dimension member positions (the oriented leading
coordinate of the fitted space) correlate with first-dimension
DW-NOMINATE at $r = .98$, and per-congress with Nokken--Poole scores
\citep{nokken2004congressional} at $r = \npRmin$--$\npRmax$, so the
improvement in fit does not come at the cost of the scale's
interpretation.

\subsection{Forecasting future votes}

Table~\ref{tab:regime} reports the temporal-forecast comparison, in
which every model predicts the final ten percent of each
congress-chamber's rollcalls from the preceding ninety. The ensemble
reaches \champForecastLL{} log loss (GMP \champForecastGMP;
\champForecastAcc\% accuracy, \champContestedAcc\% on contested
votes, APRE \champAPRE) on \forecastTestN{} strictly future votes.
The comparison set is instructive at both ends. A constant-probability
baseline scores \constantForecastLL{}; a member-by-question table of
empirical voting rates scores \memberqForecastLL{}; and the
2011--2016 text-based architectures, reimplemented with modern
optimizers and embeddings where applicable, fail to improve on that
table under proper scoring (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:lit})---the
Gerrish--Blei-style ideal-point-topic model, scored as published,
performs worse than the constant baseline (\gbRawForecastLL{}), and
recovers only to \gbTcalForecastLL{} with temperature calibration.
Those reimplementations do not exhaust what their era's methods might
achieve under indefinite tuning, but the gap between them and the
present pipeline is large. Because the ensemble's TF--IDF component
includes CRS-curated subject metadata whose revision history cannot
be verified (Appendix~\ref{app:audit}), we report throughout both the
ensemble (\champForecastLL) and the fully leakage-clean single
sentence-encoder tower (\incForecastLL); every qualitative conclusion
in this paper holds under the conservative number. Paired
rollcall-cluster bootstraps place the ensemble's margin over its own
strongest component at $[\pairedDeltaOneLo, \pairedDeltaOneHi]$ log
loss, so the gains are not artifacts of vote clustering.

Three stratifications bound what the forecasting result does and does
not mean. First, because bills often generate multiple rollcalls, a
model could in principle recall a bill family's coalition from
training rather than infer it from text. The opposite holds: on the
\freshRC{} held-out rollcalls whose bills were never voted on in the
training window, the text model's advantage over its no-text
counterpart is \emph{larger} (log loss \freshLLchamp{} against
\freshLLnotext) than on the \seenRC{} rollcalls with prior votes on
the same bill (\seenLLchamp{} against \seenLLnotext)---the gains come
from reading new bills, not remembering old ones. Second, the text
gain is concentrated in the House (\houseLLchamp{} against
\houseLLnotext{} without text) and much smaller in the Senate
(\senateLLchamp{} against \senateLLnotext), a chamber difference that
recurs in Section~\ref{sec:protest} and that we report as a boundary
rather than smooth over. Third, the probabilities are usable as
probabilities: expected calibration error is \eceChamp{} for the
ensemble (Brier \brierChamp; reliability curves in
Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:calibration}), against \eceNotext{}
(\brierNotext) for the no-text counterpart.

Because iterative development can overfit even a temporal holdout,
the design includes a test that iteration cannot contaminate: a
predecessor artifact was frozen and hash-pinned on June 12, 2026, and
subsequently scored on the \prospectiveN{} member-vote decisions
Congress cast after its June 9 data snapshot. It recorded
\prospectiveVoneLL{} log loss (\prospectiveVoneAcc\% accuracy) on the
post-snapshot sample and \postFreezeLL{} on the \postFreezeN{}
decisions cast strictly after the freeze date itself, both consistent
with its development-time performance (Table~\ref{tab:prospective}).
The audit is preliminary---\prospectiveRC{} rollcalls is a small
cluster count, and the interval in Table~\ref{tab:prospective} is
correspondingly wide---but it accumulates mechanically as Congress
votes, and the current ensemble is frozen on the same terms.

\section{Text Anticipates Majority-Party Defections}
\label{sec:protest}

Our observable in this section is the majority-party
\emph{defection}: a majority member voting nay when the party's
majority votes yea. In the framework of Section~\ref{sec:theory},
defections are the sharpest facts about agenda-conditional
coalitions---the members a given situation costs the leadership. The
category includes sincere disagreement and constituency pressure, and
also the protest votes that are the scaling literature's newest
measurement problem: majority members voting against bills they
arguably prefer \citep{fowler2026protest}, whom one-dimensional
models can misplace when they appear to side with the opposite
pole---the ends-against-the-middle patterns that
\citet{duckmayr2023ends} model with a generalized response function.
Existing treatments in both traditions are retrospective,
reinterpreting votes after they are cast. This section shows that
defections are predictable before the vote, then examines how: which
features of the text carry the signal, and where text alone falls
short. The claim that the predicted defections include the protest
episodes of substantive interest runs through the worked cases, not
through the aggregate statistic.

\subsection{An episode}

The logic has two parts. Member history identifies \emph{who} sits on
a bill's losing flank, but history alone cannot indicate \emph{which
bills} put the flank in revolt, because that depends on what the bill
is---a leadership compromise, a bipartisan spending deal, a
suspension-calendar package. That information is in the text.
Figure~\ref{fig:protest} compares the two models on the American
Relief Act of December 2024 (H.R.~10545), the continuing resolution
that averted a government shutdown and passed 366--34 over a revolt
of the Republican right flank; the vote sits deep in the holdout
window, and neither model observed it. Without text, the architecture
prices the vote as a routine majority-party bill: Democratic support
is uncertain and the revolt is invisible. With the text, the model
recognizes the character of the bill: Democratic probabilities move
to near one, Republican probabilities decline with distance from the
chamber center, and the five members the model considers the
likeliest defectors in the entire chamber---Brecheen, Roy, Massie,
Crane, and Biggs---all voted nay.

\subsection{How the model reads the bill}

Where in the text does this information live? We answer with four
diagnostics of increasing directness: what the encoder considers
similar, what a linear probe recovers from the text alone, what
breaks when parts of the text are redacted at scale, and what moves
when specific bills' texts are counterfactually edited.

One clarification first, because it disciplines the language of this
section. The December 2024 continuing resolution appeared the day
before its vote, so no summary existed: the model's entire text input
was the vote question and the title---``On Motion to Suspend the
Rules and Pass. American Relief Act, 2025.'' That is also all a
contemporaneous observer could have read, and it proves sufficient
for Figure~\ref{fig:protest}; but for such late-arriving bills
``reading the bill'' means reading the title and the procedural
posture. Most of the holdout is richer: 75\% of legislation-linked
held-out rollcalls carry a genuine pre-vote summary, and the
diagnostics below use both kinds of case. Nor are the title-only
cases weak ones: on title-only holdout rollcalls the champion's log
loss is \covtitleonlyChamp{} against \covtitleonlyNotext{} without
text, while on rollcalls with no bill link at all the two models are
nearly indistinguishable (\covdesconlyChamp{} against
\covdesconlyNotext; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:benchcard})---the text
gain appears exactly where there is text to read, at whatever
length.

The first diagnostic asks what the encoder considers similar.
Table~\ref{tab:neighbors} lists, for three held-out votes of the
118th Congress, the nearest training-window rollcalls in
sentence-embedding space, along with each neighbor's realized
majority-party defection share. The December 2024 continuing
resolution's neighborhood consists of earlier must-pass fiscal
packages---the Tax Relief for American Families and Workers Act, the
bipartisan tax agreement that drew a 22\% majority defection; a
disaster tax-relief bill; and an earlier continuing-appropriations
extension that drew a 42\% defection. The encoder, in other words,
places the bill among precisely the earlier bills that provoked the
same right-flank revolts, and the model's training on those
neighbors' votes is what prices the defection risk. The BIOSECURE
Act, a China-focused measure that passed with near-unanimous majority
support, sits instead among low-defection national-security and
regulatory bills. The second diagnostic removes members from the
picture entirely: a ridge regression of each training rollcall's
majority-defection share on its text embedding alone, evaluated on
the holdout window, predicts realized defection shares at
$r = \revoltProbeR$ across \revoltProbeN{} majority-side passage
votes. Bill text by itself, with no member information, carries a
measurable revolt signal.

The table's middle panel is equally instructive because there text
similarity fails. The Social Security Fairness Act drew a 34\%
majority defection, yet its embedding neighbors are consensual
benefit expansions that drew almost none: nothing in the summary's
language marks the bill as contentious, because the conflict was
procedural---the bill reached the floor by discharge petition rather
than through the majority leadership's agenda. The full model
nevertheless ranks the \ssfaDefectors{} Republican defectors on this
vote better than its no-text twin (within-vote AUC \ssfaAUCtext{}
against \ssfaAUCnotext), drawing on member history and institutional
features rather than content.

The third diagnostic redacts the text at scale.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} feeds the frozen champion's sentence-encoder
tower systematically corrupted versions of the exact text template it
was trained on, for all \ablRC{} legislation-linked holdout rollcalls,
holding every non-text feature fixed; only the embedding input
changes, so differences isolate the text component. Three results
structure the table. Removing the vote question costs the most
($\ablOrigLL \rightarrow \ablNoQLL$ log loss)---the procedural
posture of a vote is the single most informative phrase the model
reads, consistent with the discharge-petition case above. The placebo
is the sharpest test: replacing a bill's description and summary with
those of a \emph{different} bill in the same policy area and question
bucket costs more ($\ablShuffLL$) than deleting the summary outright
($\ablNoSumLL$)---topic-matched but wrong-bill text actively misleads
the model, so the signal is bill-specific content, not policy-area
membership. Replacing every title with generic language costs
$\ablGenTitleLL$, pooling across the corpus the title effect that
Table~\ref{tab:counterfactual} demonstrates bill by bill. And masking
proper nouns costs little ($\ablNoPropLL$) while stripping digits
costs nothing: the model is not recognizing names or dates. A policy-area decomposition
(Appendix Table~\ref{tab:policygain}) locates the text gains where
legislative language is most distinctive---government operations,
international affairs, public lands---and near zero for taxation and
commemorative business.

The fourth diagnostic edits individual bills.
Table~\ref{tab:counterfactual} performs counterfactual edits on two
exemplars through the same frozen tower. For the title-only
continuing resolution, replacing the title with generic language cuts
the predicted majority defection from \cfAraOrig\% to \cfAraGeneric\%

question from suspension to ordinary passage \emph{raises} predicted
defection to \cfAraPassage\%, the model having learned that
leadership brings consensus business to the suspension calendar. For
a summary-rich case---the 2006 Appalachian Regional Development
amendments, a regional spending authorization whose 25\% right-flank
revolt the model largely anticipated---substituting a same-area but
different bill's summary halves the predicted revolt
(\cfArdaOrig\% to \cfArdaSwap\%), while deleting interior sentences
that mention spending programs, with the title retained, moves the
prediction almost nowhere (\cfArdaCut\%). Together with the
redaction table, the picture of \emph{how} the model reads is
consistent and honest: it reads the way a hurried professional
does---procedural posture first, then the title and the leading
language of the summary, with the fine print contributing least.

\subsection{The result generalizes}

Define the detection task on the \protRC{} held-out rollcalls in
which a member's party majority voted yea and at least three majority
members defected (\protN{} defections in total): rank that party's
members by predicted defection probability, compute the AUC within
each rollcall, and average with equal weight across rollcalls. The
text-reading ensemble attains \protAUCtext{}, against \protAUCnotext{}
for its no-text counterpart---text resolves roughly a fifth of the
remaining ranking errors. The result is robust in the directions a
skeptic would probe. The fully leakage-clean single tower attains
\protAUCclean, so the gain does not depend on the CRS-curated
metadata discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:audit}. Dropping the
three-defection filter and averaging over all \protRCall{}
positive-defection rollcalls gives \protAUCtextAll{} against
\protAUCnotextAll. Predicted defection \emph{shares} track realized
shares across rollcalls (predicted--realized correlation
\defShareRChamp{} against \defShareRNotext{} without text), though
the highest-decile predictions overshoot---the model flags the right
votes but overstates how large the largest revolts will be
(Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:calibration}b). The one boundary is the
chamber split: the text gain lives in the House
(\protAUCtextHouse{} against \protAUCnotextHouse) and is absent in
the Senate (\protAUCtextSenate{} against \protAUCnotextSenate{} over
\protRCsenate{} rollcalls), where the negotiated floor agenda gives
bill text little marginal purchase---a finding we report as a limit
of the instrument, not a footnote.

Figure~\ref{fig:capture} pools the evidence that
Figure~\ref{fig:protest} presents for one vote. Panel (a) plots the
within-vote detection statistic vote by vote: the text model is
higher on 70\% of rollcalls, and its gains concentrate exactly where
member history fails---the cloud of votes whose no-text AUC falls
below 0.6, the coalition-scrambling situations, is pulled up
substantially by the text. Panel (b) states the practical meaning: a
whip counting heads who screens majority members in the model's
predicted-risk order finds a given share of the eventual defectors
several members sooner than under the no-text ranking, and both
orderings dominate chance by a wide margin.

Two connections tie the result to the measurement literature, one of
them chastened by its own check. The canonical hard cases of
Figure~\ref{fig:membergmp} are also members whose defections the
text model prices well---Paul and Amash sit at the
\paulDefGainPct th and \amashDefGainPct th percentiles of the

overlap test shows the two improvements are essentially uncorrelated
across the full membership (Spearman $\rho = \overlapRho$ over
\overlapN{} members): the scaling gains and the defection gains are
related at the level of the phenomenon, not member by member, and we
claim no more. And the ensemble's largest remaining
residuals---defections
inconsistent with even the full behavioral record---are released as a
ranked ledger, a sampling frame enriched for pressure and defection
episodes, for studying the votes that even text cannot explain.

\subsection{A pre-vote measure of party stress}

The section's output is not only a fit statistic; it is a usable
measure. Figure~\ref{fig:revoltrisk} treats the predicted
majority-defection share as a pre-vote index of stress on party
government and asks how it performed over one substantively
interesting stretch: every majority-yea passage-family vote of the
118th House holdout window, the final months of the Johnson
speakership. Each point is one vote, predicted share against realized
share; the binned means rise monotonically, meaning the index sorts
the agenda by revolt risk before any votes are cast. The votes it
flags as risky, and the votes that in fact blew up, are the same
kind: continuing resolutions and the lands-, recognition-, and
commemoration-type spending items on which the Republican right
flank habitually defects. The model understates the extremes---the
largest realized revolts sit above the diagonal, consistent with the
calibration result of Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:calibration}b---so the
index is a risk ordering, not a point forecast of magnitude. A
researcher of party government gains something existing measures do
not offer: party unity rates, roll rates, and NOMINATE-based indices
all summarize stress after the votes are cast, while this index
prices each agenda item as it arrives on the floor.

\section{Text Predicts Bill Cutpoints}
\label{sec:cutpoints}

The bill-location literature recovers proposal and status-quo
positions from realized votes and cosponsorships
\citep{peress2013estimating, richman2011parties}---necessarily after
the fact. If bill content organizes coalitions, however, a bill's
text should locate it before anyone votes. Our target is the
\emph{realized rollcall cutpoint}: the cutpoint and direction of each
vote under the per-chamber-congress one-dimensional fits of
Section~\ref{sec:setup} (the eight-dimensional completion model plays
no role here). It is a property of the realized vote on the realized
agenda---reflecting content, agenda control, and party pressure
together---not the proposal or status-quo location of the
bill-location literature. We hold out the final twenty percent of
each congress-chamber's rollcalls and predict both quantities from
strictly pre-vote features; the figure and stratified table exclude
the small number of rollcalls whose realized cutpoints fall beyond
four member standard deviations.

Figure~\ref{fig:cutpred} and Table~\ref{tab:cutpred} report the
result. On the \cutTestN{} identified held-out rollcalls, text plus
metadata predicts cutpoint locations with a mean absolute error of
\cutComboMAE{} member-standard-deviations and a predicted-realized
correlation of $r = \cutComboR$, against a no-feature baseline of
\cutConstMAE{} (and $r = 0$); it classifies coalition direction
correctly for \dirCombo\% of rollcalls, against a
majority-direction baseline of \dirConst\%. The headline feature set
here contains only the sentence-embedding representation of pre-vote
text plus institutional metadata; the CRS-revisable subject fields
discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:audit} enter only the separately
reported TF--IDF row. Panel~(a) shows the predictions
member-standard-deviation by member-standard-deviation: mass
concentrates along the diagonal, with the labeled landmark bills
indicating the scale's interpretation---bills realized to cut deep in
the majority's territory are predicted there.

The decomposition in panels (b) and (c) assigns each input its role.
Institutional metadata---question type, the bill sponsor's party,
and, on amendment votes, the amender's party, with their
interactions---is a strong baseline on its own: it locates cutpoints
with $\mathrm{MAE} = \cutMetaMAE$ and supplies a default coalition
side correct \dirMeta\% of the time, which is what one would expect
in a chamber whose agenda is set by the majority party. Text improves
every metric beyond that baseline, with the largest gains in
direction (\dirCombo\%) and location correlation ($\cutMetaR
\rightarrow \cutComboR$). The interpretation is the same division of
labor as in Section~\ref{sec:protest}: the acting legislator's party
indicates which side a bill is probably on, and the content indicates
when that default is wrong, and by how much. Among the text
components (Table~\ref{tab:cutpred}, bottom panel), the bill summary
carries most of the directional information and the vote question
most of the locational information.

Figure~\ref{fig:dirterms} examines the directional signal at the term
level, using an interpretable sparse model fit to final-passage
summaries only. The language of program-building---\emph{establish},
\emph{grants}, \emph{assistance}, \emph{workers},
\emph{emergency}---is associated with liberal-yea coalitions, and the
language of restraint and revision---\emph{rule}, \emph{regulation},
\emph{permit}, \emph{budget}, \emph{injury}---with conservative-yea
ones. These are predictive associations on a leader-curated agenda,
not causal claims about language; but they indicate that the
embedding models' directional signal rests on substantively sensible
regularities rather than incidental correlates.

Because the prediction target is itself an estimate, two
stratifications gauge how much of the residual error belongs to the
target rather than the prediction
(Appendix Table~\ref{tab:cutstrata}). Realized cutpoints are pinned
most precisely where rollcall discrimination is strong; on the
high-discrimination half of the holdout, the mean absolute error
falls to \cutHiDiscMAE{} member-standard-deviations and the
predicted--realized correlation rises to $r = \cutHiDiscR$, so a
substantial share of the headline error is noise in the target. By
vote type, resolutions and procedural votes are located most
accurately and cloture least; final-passage votes sit at
$r = \cutPassageR$. Two caveats bound the
exercise. The prediction target is the cutpoint of our own spatial
fit---a realized-vote quantity with estimation error of its own---and
we have not yet benchmarked the predictions against the
proposal/status-quo estimates of \citet{peress2013estimating} or
\citet{richman2011parties} on an overlapping sample; that comparison,
which requires reimplementing those estimators, is the natural next
test. An interactive forecaster operationalizes the section's result:
a user pastes a hypothetical bill's text, selects a sponsoring party
and chamber, and receives a predicted cutpoint and a member-by-member
vote forecast, with a sponsor--coalition mismatch flag for inputs off
the curated agenda (Appendix~\ref{app:dashboard}). The public
interface defaults to final-passage forecasts; amendment or
procedural forecasts are conditional on the question type and, where
applicable, amender party that the user supplies or the interface
defaults.

\section{Two Boundaries the Framework Predicts}
\label{sec:limits}

Users of these methods should know where they stop. Both boundaries
are measured rather than conjectured, and both are commitments of the
agenda-politics account of Section~\ref{sec:theory} rather than
anomalies. The first concerns
\emph{majority transitions}. At every House majority flip in the
window---and at none of the placebo transitions---the
procedural-vote component of member history stops transferring: the
model's procedural log loss averages \flipProcLL{} across flips
against \placeboProcLL{} across placebos, while final-passage
transfer is flip-invariant (\flipPassLL{} against \placeboPassLL).
The all-flips/no-placebos statement refers to the individual
transition losses plotted in Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:transitions};
the averages summarize that event-level comparison. This
member-history component, then, captures procedural role that changes
with majority control, not only stable preference, and scores
estimated in one majority configuration should be carried across
configurations only with care \citep{bateman2017house}. The second
concerns \emph{amendments}. Supplying amendments' purpose text makes
prediction worse, not better, in pre-registered, placebo-controlled
experiments (Appendix~\ref{app:ledger}); what moves amendment
prediction is actor identity---the amender's party takes direction
accuracy from approximately a coin flip to 77\%. In these amendment
models, purpose text provides no useful incremental signal and can
degrade forecasts once bill identity and procedural context are
modeled---a measured limit on what amendment-purpose language
reveals, in the representations tested here.

\section{Conclusion}

What modern language models contribute to the measurement of Congress
is, on our evidence, a measurement of agenda politics: the pre-vote
text of a bill identifies the situation its arrival creates, and a
model that reads it can price the coalition that situation
produces---before the vote, member by member, with the signal
traceable to specific parts of the text. That capacity yields the
three results documented here: a fit standard that improves on the
classical measure exactly where it struggles (a vote-only result that
disciplines interpretation of the rest); advance detection of the
majority defections, including canonical protest episodes, that
scaling models could previously only reinterpret; and prospective
locations for bills on the realized agenda that the bill-location
literature could previously estimate only in retrospect. The same
account predicts where the instrument stops---the negotiated Senate
floor, majority transitions, amendment language---and those
boundaries are measured, not conjectured. The benchmark, the frozen
artifacts whose forecasting claims the future continues to audit, the
interactive forecaster, and a complete ledger of what failed along
the way are released together.

\section{Measurement Provenance}
\label{app:provenance}

Which estimator produces each quantity:
the ensemble produces vote forecasts (pre-vote), the protest-detection
scores (pre-vote), and the out-of-character residual ledger
(in-sample, labeled as such); per chamber-congress spatial fits
produce positions, cutpoints, and directions (from realized votes);
ridge and logistic models on pre-vote features produce the cutpoint
\emph{predictions} of Section~\ref{sec:cutpoints}. An identifiability
remark bounds the enterprise: if two member-rollcall states are
observationally equivalent given the full available information
set---inducing the same joint distribution of votes and observed
covariates---no measure computed from that information can
distinguish them. The
repository's synthetic suite demonstrates the constructive direction:
planted structures are recovered exactly by the models whose held-out
scores improve.

\section{Feature Timing and Text Coverage}
\label{app:audit}

Every input and its timestamp rule: vote question and description
(fixed at the vote); bill title and sponsor (introduction); bill
summaries (only versions dated strictly before the vote); amendment
purpose and amender party (filed at submission; joined through
recorded-vote references with session-offset correction);
member-by-question rates (training window only); majority status
(member composition); early stopping and calibration (final 5\% of
training rollcalls, inside train). Two fields are generated at the
vote itself and admit no earlier timestamp: the Clerk's vote question
and vote description. A reader who wants the model's performance
under a strictly-before-the-day information set should consult the
redaction ablations of Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, whose
question-removed and summary-only rows bound the contribution of
at-the-vote fields; bill summaries themselves enter only with CRS
action dates strictly before the vote date, so no summary can
postdate the vote it predicts. CRS-revisable policy metadata is
excluded from the leakage-clean text; the ensemble's TF--IDF
component includes it, so the fully conservative headline is the
single MiniLM tower's \incForecastLL{} rather than the ensemble's
\champForecastLL. Text coverage: the vote question is universal; 95\%
of legislation-linked rollcalls have a pre-vote summary or title;
\amdtFilledNoDesc{} of \amdtNoDesc{} text-starved amendment rollcalls
gain amendment-side information via the records join.

\section{Model Specification}
\label{app:spec}

The champion is a logistic blend (weights 0.57/0.12/0.42, fit on the
temporal development slice) of three two-tower models: member
embedding $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$ and intercept; rollcall
parameters amortized from features by a two-layer MLP (width 128);
sponsor-alignment and member-by-question logit offsets; temperature
calibration on the development slice. Towers differ in text features:
MiniLM (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, frozen, 384d, 1,500-character template);
plus TF--IDF/SVD of title-subject metadata; and Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B
(1,024d, 6,000 characters). AdamW (lr .02, wd $10^{-4}$), batch
$2^{17}$, early stopping on the temporal slice. The blend weights are
unconstrained logistic-stacking coefficients (they need not sum to
one). The no-text counterpart deletes all text blocks and is
independently trained, early-stopped, and temperature-calibrated
under the identical protocol; nothing is inherited from the text
models. One
pipeline command regenerates every exhibit from raw Voteview and
BILLSTATUS files; the headline table costs under two GPU-hours on a
laptop-class accelerator.

\section{The Interactive Forecaster}
\label{app:dashboard}

A single-tower approximation of the frozen champion (MiniLM component

record carries the user's sponsoring party; the cutpoint comes from a
fractional-response logistic fit of predicted probabilities on
current-member positions, with fitted mean $q_i =
\sigma(\alpha + \beta x_i)$; the reported cutpoint $x^* =
-\alpha/\beta$ is the 0.5 crossing of the fitted curve, displayed
only when the slope is meaningful and the crossing lies within the
chamber. Two diagnostics accompany every forecast. Inputs are scored
for support---cosine distance to the nearest training-corpus bills in
embedding space---and flagged when they lie far from anything that
has reached the floor, since Section~\ref{sec:theory}'s account
implies the model is validated only on the curated agenda. And
forecasts whose predicted coalition conflicts with the bill's own
sponsorship carry a sponsor--coalition mismatch flag; such
extrapolations are unvalidated. Formal prediction intervals for the
displayed cutpoint (rollcall-cluster conformal residuals) are queued
for the public release.
