\begin{abstract}
\noindent Nearly everything political science measures about members of
Congress---ideology, loyalty, issue positions---is extracted from models
that predict rollcall votes. Prediction quality therefore bounds
measurement quality in an informational sense: structure a model cannot
predict is structure no measure derived from it can contain---though,
as this paper's final section shows, the converse does not hold, and
separating predictive signal from construct-valid measurement is
itself part of the work. We build the strongest vote predictor on our
benchmark---an ensemble
reading rollcall-level text with modern language-model encoders,
anchored on member histories, with calibrated probabilities
(\champForecastAcc\% accuracy on strictly future votes;
\champContestedAcc\% on contested votes; its frozen, hash-pinned
predecessor scored \prospectiveVoneAcc\% on the \prospectiveN{} votes
Congress cast in the three weeks \emph{after} that artifact was
finalized, and the ensemble's own prospective ledger is running)---and
then use it as a measurement instrument. The model's
internals recover and extend the classical measurement canon: positions
correlating at $r=.98$ with DW-NOMINATE, party-loyalty residuals that
identify the chamber's mavericks by name, issue-specific positions that
recover libertarian non-interventionism from votes alone, bill-level
cutpoints locating each rollcall on the ideological scale, and a new
measure of \emph{out-of-character votes}. Just as informative is what
the instrument cannot do. Amendment coalitions resist text-based
prediction because they form around deals rather than content---a
measurable boundary on what legislative language reveals. And no model
survives a change in majority control: member histories actively
mislead across the 2022 House flip, showing that much of what vote
models measure is institutional \emph{role}, not stable preference.
Career-spanning NOMINATE scores, by contrast, nearly match the deep
model's cross-congress accuracy---a genuine vindication of traditional
measurement design. We release the benchmark, models, and a
pre-registered ledger of all results, positive and negative.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The empirical study of Congress rests on measurement, and the
measurement rests on prediction. An ideal point is the parameter of a
model built to predict votes \citep{poole1997congress,
clinton2004statistical}; a party-unity score is a prediction from party
membership; an interest-group rating is a prediction from a hand-picked
vote subset. The field's measures of legislators are, almost without
exception, the interpretable residue of vote-prediction machinery. This
has a bracing implication that the measurement literature rarely states:
\emph{how well the machinery predicts is an upper bound on how much the
measures can know.} Structure a model fails to predict is structure no
measure derived from it can contain.

For four decades the machinery in question has been the spatial voting
model, and it earned its dominance: one or two dimensions classify
around ninety percent of votes in the modern Congress. The past
fifteen years of scaling research has pushed on the machinery from
every side: legislator-specific unpredictability
\citep{lauderdale2010unpredictable}, issue-specific dimensions
estimated with topic-weighted votes \citep{lauderdale2014scaling,
crespin2010dimensions}, joint models of votes and speech
\citep{kim2018estimating}, computational estimators for massive data
\citep{imai2016fast}, supervised learning as a measurement bridge
\citep{bonica2018inferring}, bill and status-quo locations for testing
theories of lawmaking \citep{peress2013estimating, richman2011parties},
and, most recently, models built to rescue the members whom
one-dimensional scaling visibly mis-measures---ideological extremists
who vote with the opposite pole against the middle
\citep{duckmayr2023ends} and majority members who cast protest votes
against bills they prefer \citep{fowler2026protest}. A parallel
literature at the intersection of machine learning and legislative
studies tried to read the bills themselves \citep{gerrish2011predicting,
gerrish2012they, kraft2016embedding}, but worked with the text tools of
its era and went quiet before modern language models existed. Today's
encoders read legislative text incomparably better, and prediction
bounds measurement. Do better predictors deliver richer, sharper, more
trustworthy measures of Congress? What new things can we now measure?
And where does even the best modern predictor hit a wall---and what do
the walls teach us about the institution?

This paper answers in three movements. \emph{First, we build and
validate the instrument} (Section~\ref{sec:instrument}). On an open
benchmark of 10.6 million voting decisions (101st--119th Congresses),
we develop an ensemble model that reads rollcall-level text with
sentence encoders, maps it to vote-choice parameters through deep
heads, anchors on each member's behavioral history, and reports
calibrated probabilities. On strictly future votes within a congress it
reaches \champForecastAcc\% accuracy (\champContestedAcc\% on contested
votes; log loss \champForecastLL), far beyond both the classical
spatial model and the 2011--2016 text-based models, which---under
proper scoring rules and honest temporal splits---never actually beat a
simple table of member voting rates (Table~\ref{tab:regime}). Because a
measurement instrument is only as credible as its evaluation, we score
every model under four regimes of increasing difficulty and add a fifth
test immune to researcher iteration by construction: models frozen and
hash-pinned, then scored on the \prospectiveN{} votes Congress cast in
the following weeks. The frozen model's prospective performance
(\prospectiveVoneLL{} log loss, \prospectiveVoneAcc\% accuracy) matches
its development performance, certifying that the numbers here are not
artifacts of adaptive overfitting.

\emph{Second, we measure} (Sections~\ref{sec:members}
and~\ref{sec:bills}). From the instrument's internals we extract, and
validate against external benchmarks, a suite of member- and bill-level
measures. Learned member positions correlate with DW-NOMINATE's first
dimension at $r = .98$ (Figure~\ref{fig:validation})---the sanity
check---and then go further. A \emph{party-loyalty residual} isolates
discipline from ideology and independently rediscovers the canonical
mavericks of the last decade---Walter Jones, Justin Amash, Collin
Peterson, Kyrsten Sinema---from votes alone
(Figure~\ref{fig:loyalty}). \emph{Issue-specific positions} recover,
without any text about the members, the libertarian non-interventionist
bloc (dovish on defense, civil-libertarian on crime, otherwise very
conservative) and the hawkish wing of the Democratic party
(Figure~\ref{fig:issuegrid}). At the bill level, every rollcall acquires a
\emph{cutpoint}---the location on the ideological scale where a member
becomes indifferent---and the distribution of cutpoints against the
distribution of members maps the strategic structure of the agenda:
which policy areas divide the parties at the center, which split one
party internally, which pass by consensus
(Figure~\ref{fig:cutpoints}). And because the model outputs calibrated
probabilities for every vote, its largest errors constitute a new
dataset of \emph{out-of-character votes}---defections the best
available behavioral model could not see coming
(Figure~\ref{fig:surprises})---a systematic starting point for studying
pressure, persuasion, and idiosyncratic representation.

\emph{Third, we measure the walls} (Section~\ref{sec:limits}). Two
boundaries survived every attempt we pre-registered against them, and
both are substantive findings about Congress rather than engineering
shortfalls. Amendment votes carry a third of the model's remaining
error, yet supplying amendments' actual purpose text makes prediction
\emph{worse}, not better---a result we establish with a
placebo-controlled design. Within a bill, the model exploits the
correlation of the amendment series more effectively than the
amendments' content: amendment coalitions form around deals, not
language. That is a measured boundary on what legislative text
reveals---content organizes coalitions on final passage, context
organizes them on amendments. Second, when majority control of the
House changed in 2022, the member-history features learned within the
prior majority configuration partially inverted: pooled member records
\emph{anti-transfer} across the flip (a member who reliably supported
procedural motions in the majority reliably opposes them in the
minority), our best model posts higher log loss on the unseen congress
than a three-way count table that merely knows who holds the chamber,
and a simple logit on frozen DW-NOMINATE scores nearly matches the
deep model's transfer accuracy (\nomctxCongressoutAcc\% versus
\champCongressoutAcc\%). The lesson is
constitutional in the small: a large share of what any vote-based
measure captures is institutional role, not stable preference, and
measures built within one majority configuration should be carried
across configurations only with care. That career-spanning NOMINATE
scores---estimated from behavior in both roles---are the most durable
object in our entire horse race is a quiet vindication of the
traditional design.

Throughout, we practice the evaluation discipline the measurement
stakes demand: every development experiment was pre-registered in a
public ledger with hypothesis and falsification test written before the
run, selection touched validation data only, the final model comparison
observed test sets exactly once, and negative results are reported with
their mechanisms. The benchmark, code, frozen artifacts, and ledger are
public; a companion paper develops the member-level measures at full
depth.

\section{The Instrument: Prediction You Can Build Measures On}
\label{sec:instrument}

\subsection{Data and task}

We predict individual voting decisions: given everything observable
before a rollcall, output each member's probability of voting yea. The
panel covers every yea/nay decision in the 101st through 119th
Congresses from Voteview \citep{lewis2026voteview}---10.6 million
decisions---with bill-side information (titles, policy areas, dated
summary versions, sponsors, amendment purposes) from government
BILLSTATUS records, which begin with the 108th Congress. The scope
division is strict and worth stating once: \emph{every forecasting
result in this paper uses the text-era congresses (108th--119th)
only}; the pre-text congresses enter only the completion-regime
spatial fits, which use no text. Rollcall-level text is assembled
strictly from information available before each vote; the
feature-timing audit and text-coverage accounting are in
Appendix~\ref{app:audit}. Splits are deterministic and content-hashed;
a common harness strips labels before any model sees evaluation rows.

\subsection{Measurement definitions}
\label{sec:defs}

Because the paper's contribution is measurement, the derived quantities
are defined here once, precisely. The model outputs $\hat p_{iv}$, the
probability member $i$ votes yea on rollcall $v$, from features dated
strictly before $v$ (the feature-timing audit is
Appendix~\ref{app:audit}). The \emph{spatial fits} estimate
$P(\text{yea}) = \sigma(a_v x_i + b_v + c_i)$ per chamber-congress with
party-sign initialization; a rollcall's \emph{cutpoint} is
$-(b_v + \bar c)/a_v$, reported only when $|a_v| \geq 0.35$ (the
location is unidentified for near-valence votes), and its
\emph{direction} is $\mathrm{sign}(a_v)$. The \emph{loyalty residual}
is $\bar y_i^{U} - \overline{\hat p}_i^{U}$ over member $i$'s
party-unity votes $U$ (majorities of the two parties opposed), where
the expectation comes from the spatial fit; members need at least 100
unity votes. \emph{Issue-specific positions} refit the spatial model
within each policy area (fifty votes minimum per member-area) and are
reported as deviations from the member's all-votes position after
per-area standardization. The \emph{out-of-character score} of a
realized vote $y_{iv}$ is $-\log \hat p_{iv}(y_{iv})$ under the frozen
ensemble; Section~3.4 states its in-sample estimand explicitly. All
quantities are estimated from, and therefore conditional on, the
\emph{realized rollcall agenda}: they describe behavior on the votes
leaders chose to hold, a conditioning we return to throughout.

\subsection{Why evaluation regimes are measurement questions}

A model's fitness as a measurement instrument depends on \emph{what
kind} of prediction it is good at, so we evaluate under regimes with
different and progressively stricter information constraints: \emph{completion} (random individual votes held
out, same-rollcall votes observable---the psychometric setting in which
ideal points are traditionally assessed); \emph{random rollcall
holdout} (whole rollcalls held out, interleaved in time---the
2011--2016 literature's setting); \emph{temporal forecast} (the final
ten percent of each congress-chamber's rollcalls---a true forecaster's
setting); \emph{congress-out transfer} (train through the 116th
Congress, predict the 118th, across the majority flip); and
\emph{prospective} (hash-pinned frozen models scored only on votes that
occur after freezing, following the logic of the reusable holdout
problem \citep{dwork2015holdout}). Our primary metric is log loss---a
proper scoring rule that rewards honest probabilities---because
measurement consumers use the probabilities, not just the
classifications; accuracy, AUC, contested-vote accuracy, and APRE are
reported throughout for comparability.

throughout: per-congress positions correlate with Nokken--Poole scores
\citep{nokken2004congressional} at $r = \npRmin$--$\npRmax$ across
recent chamber-congresses. Still, completion-regime performance should
never be quoted as ``prediction.''

\subsection{The champion and its certification}

The final model is a logistic blend of three two-tower networks. Each
tower embeds members in a latent space and amortizes each rollcall's
choice parameters from its text---the vote question, the vote
description, and the latest pre-vote bill summary, encoded by modern
sentence encoders (the three towers vary the encoder and text
representation)---anchored by a member-by-question-type empirical rate
and calibrated on a temporal internal slice of training data.
Development followed a pre-registered ledger; the ablation path and the
full set of negative results (bigger encoders, recency weighting,
within-bill context features, per-bucket calibration) are in
Appendix~\ref{app:ledger} with their diagnosed mechanisms.

On the temporal forecast test---\forecastTestN{} future votes---the
blend reaches \champForecastLL{} log loss, \champForecastAcc\%
accuracy, \champContestedAcc\% contested accuracy, APRE \champAPRE.
For certification beyond any possibility of test-set contamination, we
are exact about which artifact certifies what. The single-tower
\emph{predecessor} (v1) was frozen on June 12, 2026 (weights
hash-pinned, fit through the June 9 data snapshot) and scored
\prospectiveVoneLL{} log loss and \prospectiveVoneAcc\% accuracy on
the \prospectiveRC{} rollcalls and \prospectiveN{} votes Congress cast
June 10--30 (rollcall-cluster bootstrap 95\% interval for the log
loss: \prospLLlo--\prospLLhi). That interval is wide---43 rollcalls is
a contamination check, not a precision instrument---and its value is
what it rules out: the development-test numbers are not inflated by
adaptive iteration. The ensemble champion (v2) was frozen on July 3,
2026 on the same data snapshot; scored on the same window it ties the
predecessor on log loss with one point higher accuracy, and its
pristine window (votes after July 3) accumulates going forward. All
regime-audit and measurement results in this paper come from the
ensemble; the prospective certificate quoted in the abstract belongs
to the predecessor, with the ensemble's ledger running.

\section{Measuring Members}
\label{sec:members}

\subsection{Positions, validated}

The instrument's member representations should first reproduce what we
already trust. Fitting the benchmark's one-dimensional spatial model
per chamber (with the identification conventions documented in the
repository), learned positions correlate with DW-NOMINATE's first
dimension at $r = .98$ in both chambers---and at $.84$--$.91$
\emph{within} party, where the information is
(Figure~\ref{fig:validation}). Nothing about DW-NOMINATE entered the
estimation; the agreement is two instruments discovering the same
object.

\subsection{Loyalty beyond ideology}

Ideology and discipline are conceptually distinct---a centrist who
always answers the whip differs from a centrist who defects---but
one-dimensional scores conflate them. Using the instrument, we compute
each member's \emph{expected} behavior on party-unity votes given their
position, and define the \emph{loyalty residual} as actual minus
expected unity-vote support. Figure~\ref{fig:loyalty} shows the
recent-era distribution for every eligible member under the caption's
inclusion rule (at least 100 unity votes; territorial delegates
excluded), and its two systematic properties. The distribution is
tight and asymmetric: most members are within a point or two of their
position-predicted loyalty, with a long defector tail and almost no
excess-loyalty tail. The pattern is consistent with a loyalty ceiling
enforced by party pressure, though part of the asymmetry is
mechanical---when expected support is near one, positive residuals are
bounded near zero---so the informative reading is the defector tail,
not the missing loyal one. The residual is also essentially
uncorrelated with ideological extremity, measured as the absolute
ideal point ($\rho = -.05$): the measure captures a dimension of
behavior that position does not. The labeled extremes---who emerge
from the full distribution rather than from selection---are the
members journalists and whips would name: Walter Jones as the era's
outstanding maverick, then Justin Amash, Collin Peterson, Ileana
Ros-Lehtinen. The residual is discipline net of the position variable
used in the expected-support model, though it may also absorb
preference variation---issue-specific positions, for
instance---that the overall position does not capture; with at least
100 unity votes per member, binomial sampling error is at most about
five points, several times smaller than the labeled extremes.

\subsection{Issue-specific positions}

Issue-specific structure has a distinguished lineage---appropriations
votes scale differently from the rest of the agenda
\citep{crespin2010dimensions}, and topic-weighted estimation recovers
issue variation in courts and Congress
\citep{lauderdale2014scaling}---but coverage has been the constraint.
Because every rollcall here carries a policy area from its bill's text
metadata, the spatial model can be re-estimated \emph{within} policy
areas, giving each member a position in each of the thirteen policy
domains of Table~\ref{tab:topics}---the structure a single dimension
is forced to average away. Coverage is broad: under the fifty-vote
rule, between 917 and 1,562 members receive a position in each of the
twelve legislative areas (nominations, a Senate-only domain, covers
328), and Table~\ref{tab:topics} reports the full per-topic
accounting.

Three systematic facts emerge. First, one dimension is \emph{almost}
sufficient: within-area positions correlate with overall positions at
$\rho = .95$--$.99$ (Figure~\ref{fig:issuegrid}). High correlations
alone cannot distinguish structured deviations from small random
errors; what carries the structure claim is that the deviations
recover \emph{coherent factions}---the same members deviating the same
direction across related areas, matching the qualitative record---as
documented below and in Table~\ref{tab:topics}. Second, polarization
is not uniform across the agenda (Figure~\ref{fig:topicpolar}). The
parties stand farthest apart on energy and taxation---the modern
economic core---and closest together on armed forces and international
affairs; within-party dispersion is also high in exactly those areas,
with armed forces showing the largest Democratic spread and
international affairs the largest Republican spread
(Table~\ref{tab:topics}): foreign policy is simultaneously the least
partisan and among the most internally contested terrain in both
parties. Third, the deviations recover
known ideological factions from votes alone, systematically rather
than anecdotally. Reading down Table~\ref{tab:topics}'s largest
recent-era deviators: the liberal-direction outliers on armed forces
and international affairs are the libertarian Republicans (Paul,
Crane, Roy, Sanford), civil-libertarian deviations appear on crime
(Brat, Burlison), and hawkish or border-district Democrats deviate
conservatively in the same areas. None of these members' texts,
speeches, or biographies entered the estimation; their votes alone
locate them.

\subsection{Out-of-character votes (in-sample residuals)}

A calibrated model of member behavior defines, vote by vote, what would
be surprising---and its largest realized surprises are a dataset no
existing measure provides. We are precise about the estimand: the
scores here are \emph{in-sample residuals}---the frozen champion is fit
on the member's entire record through mid-2026 and then asked about
each historical vote, so a large score means the vote is
out-of-character relative to everything the member ever did, not that
it was unforeseeable at the time. (The prospective ledger produces the
strictly forecast-based version of this measure for every vote cast
after the freeze, and will accumulate it going forward.)
Figure~\ref{fig:surprises} shows the decisions the model still gets
confidently wrong even with full hindsight. These are not model noise;
inspected, they
are recognizable episodes---high-pressure defections, district-driven
crossovers, protest votes---now identifiable systematically rather than
anecdotally. We release the full surprise ranking as a research
resource: a sampling frame for studying lobbying, leadership pressure,
and the votes that end careers.

Two clarifications locate this measure in the literature's lineage.
Relative to legislator-specific variance in a one-dimensional model
\citep{lauderdale2010unpredictable}, our unpredictability is
\emph{residual to a far richer specification}: behavior must escape
text, issue structure, party discipline, and the member's own detailed
history before it registers as surprising. And relative to protest
voting \citep{fowler2026protest}: the Squad's protest pattern is
\emph{regular}---a rich model learns it, which is exactly why those
members show ordinary loyalty residuals and no excess presence in our
surprise tail, while a one-dimensional model registers them as its
worst-fit members (Figure~\ref{fig:membergmp}). What the classical
framework treats as measurement failure is, to a sufficiently
expressive model, simply another behavioral signature; what remains in
\emph{our} surprise tail is the genuinely episodic residue.

\section{Measuring Bills}
\label{sec:bills}

The same machinery measures the \emph{agenda}. Every rollcall acquires
a discrimination (how strongly it separates the space) and a
cutpoint---the position of an indifferent member.
Figure~\ref{fig:cutpoints} plots the 118th House: the member
distribution is bimodal and the cutpoint distribution concentrates in
the empty center---the signature of an agenda organized to divide the
parties---but the policy-area breakdown shows the exceptions in
measurable form: areas whose median cutpoint sits inside a party
(internal factional fights) and areas whose cutpoints sit at the
extremes (consensus votes that separate only the flanks).

Cutpoint locations relative to the chamber's pivotal actors are the
raw material of the agenda-control literature
\citep{richman2011parties, peress2013estimating, aldrich2014polarization},
and the full-agenda measurement makes the standard quantities
immediate. In the 118th House, only \blockoutShare\% of the
\nCutHouse{} identified cutpoints fall between the floor median
(\houseFloorMed) and the majority-party median (\houseRMed)---the
region where a vote separates the majority party's own median from its
right flank---while \cutBetweenParties\% fall between the Democratic
median (\houseDMed) and the floor median: the party-line region where,
given the fitted orientation of each rollcall's discrimination, the
two parties' medians sit on opposite sides of the threshold. That
asymmetry---an agenda whose divisions run between the parties rather
than through the majority---is negative agenda control rendered as a
histogram, computed from every recorded vote of the congress rather
than a curated subset.

Text enters the measurement here: the model's text-to-parameter head is
a learned map from legislative language to conflict structure, and its
successes and failures delineate what bill content \emph{is} to a
coalition. On final-passage votes, text carries real information---the
ablation path in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:ablation} shows rollcall-level
text construction contributing some of the largest gains. On
amendments, Section~\ref{sec:limits} shows the opposite, and the
contrast is itself the measurement: language predicts coalitions where
coalitions form on content, and the places it fails are the places
procedure and bargaining, not content, do the organizing.

\subsection{One vote, walked through}

\subsection{Predicting where a bill will cut, from its text}

The dashboard's premise---that a bill's language locates it on the
scale before anyone votes---can be tested directly, and
Table~\ref{tab:cutpred} and Figure~\ref{fig:cutpred} do so. For every
rollcall in the benchmark we take the realized cutpoint and direction
(which party's side the yeas fall on) from the spatial fits, hold out
the final twenty percent of each congress-chamber's rollcalls, and ask
how well features available \emph{before} the vote predict them:
nothing; metadata (question type, sponsor party, bill category); text
alone (in classical and modern representations); or text and metadata
together. On \cutTestN{} held-out future rollcalls, text plus metadata
predicts cutpoint locations with a mean error of \cutComboMAE{}
member-standard-deviations ($r = \cutComboR$, against \cutConstMAE{}
with no features) and calls the coalition's direction correctly
\dirCombo\% of the time (chance: \dirConst\%).

The decomposition is as informative as the headline, and we report the
metadata baseline at full strength, which took two rounds of honest
reinforcement. A bill's sponsor is directional for passage votes (the
yeas fall on the sponsor's side 82 percent of the time) but a coin
flip for amendment votes, where the relevant actor is the
\emph{amender}---frequently the other party attacking the bill. The
final metadata model therefore carries both actors' parties (bill
sponsor everywhere; amender's party on the roughly three-quarters of
amendment votes recoverable from amendment records, where the
amender-side rule alone calls direction at 77 percent),
sponsor-by-question interactions, question type, bill category, and
chamber. So strengthened, metadata alone is formidable: it locates
cutpoints nearly as well as anything ($\mathrm{MAE} = \cutMetaMAE$;
\emph{where} a vote cuts is substantially institutional) and calls
direction at \dirMeta\%. Text still earns its keep on every metric:
direction rises to \dirCombo\% and the correlation of predicted with
realized locations from $r = \cutMetaR$ to $\cutComboR$. Among the
text's components, the bill summary carries the most directional
signal and the vote question the most locational signal
(Table~\ref{tab:cutpred}, bottom panel). The synthesis: institutions
pin where a vote cuts, the acting legislator's party supplies a
default side, and content is what tells you when the default is
wrong---and by how much.

\subsection{The language of left- and right-recruiting bills}

What in the text carries the directional signal? Fitting an
interpretable sparse model (TF--IDF terms, final-passage votes,
identified rollcalls, alphabetic tokens only) exposes the coefficients
directly, and Figure~\ref{fig:terms} shows the result: a lexicon of
coalition recruitment. Bills whose summaries \emph{establish programs},
\emph{authorize assistance}, and provide \emph{grants} and
\emph{services} to \emph{workers} and \emph{employees} recruit
liberal-yea coalitions; bills about \emph{rules}, \emph{regulations},
\emph{permits}, \emph{budgets}, statutory revision (\emph{sec},
\emph{code}, \emph{amends}), and \emph{injury} (the tort-reform
lexicon) recruit conservative-yea ones. The map is, recognizably, the
administrative state built versus the administrative state
checked---estimated from votes and bill summaries alone. We reached
this exhibit through three pre-registered face-validity corrections
worth recording, because each is a warning to text-as-data practice:
naive fits are dominated first by year tokens (which proxy for who
held the majority---agenda composition, not language), then by
procedural boilerplate from rules resolutions; only after restricting
to substantive passage-vote summaries does bill \emph{language} itself
speak.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{figures/direction_terms.pdf}
\caption{Coefficients of an interpretable text model predicting which
party's side a final-passage coalition falls on, bill-summary language

Because the map from text to conflict structure is an estimated object,
it can be queried with bills that do not exist. We release, alongside
the paper, an interactive forecaster: paste the text of a hypothetical
bill, name a sponsoring party and chamber, and the frozen model returns
a predicted cutpoint, party-by-party support, and each current member's
vote probability (Appendix~\ref{app:dashboard}). The tool exposes the
instrument's boundaries as deliberately as its capabilities: because
floor agendas are curated by the majority party, the model has never
observed votes on off-agenda bills, and the dashboard flags forecasts
whose internal structure reveals extrapolation---for instance, when the
predicted coalition contradicts the bill's own sponsorship. The
dashboard is thus both a research tool and a live demonstration of the
agenda-selection boundary discussed above.

\section{Measuring the Limits---and What They Say About Congress}
\label{sec:limits}

\subsection{Amendments: deals, not content}

Amendment votes carry roughly thirty percent of the instrument's
remaining error (Table~\ref{tab:decomp}), and two-thirds of amendment
rollcalls lack amendment-specific text---so supplying the amendments'
actual purpose language, parsed from BILLSTATUS records and joined
through recorded-vote references, looked like the obvious improvement.
(The join filled amendment-side information for \amdtFilledNoDesc{} of
the \amdtNoDesc{} previously text-starved amendment rollcalls, of
\amdtTotal{} total.) Pre-registered, it failed three ways, and the
failure is the finding. Corrupted joins, corrected joins, and
minimal-footprint text insertions all made prediction \emph{worse} by
similar amounts; a placebo rebuild that changed the data file without
changing content reproduced the champion exactly, exonerating the
pipeline. Mechanism: when a bill's amendments share the parent bill's
text, the model assigns the series nearly identical parameters---an
implicit pooled estimate of a correlated block. Purpose language
replaces that pooling with per-amendment, content-driven parameters
that predict worse than the bill's identity does. We state the
boundary precisely rather than expansively: \emph{short amendment
purpose text adds nothing once bill identity and procedural context
are modeled}---while actor identity adds a great deal (the amender's
party alone moves direction accuracy on amendment votes from a coin
flip to 77 percent; Section~4.1). Coalitions on amendments are
organized by who is acting on which bill at which procedural moment
far more than by what the amendment's text says---a measured boundary
of the text-as-data program that corroborates, at the level of
individual votes, accounts of amendment politics as coalition
management.

\subsection{Majority control: role, not preference}

The 118th Congress column of Table~\ref{tab:regime} is the deepest
lesson. Trained through 2021 and asked about 2023--24---across the
House majority flip---the champion posts higher log loss than a count
table keyed only on majority status, chamber, and question type
(\champCongressoutLL{} versus \majorityCongressoutLL); pooled
member histories are outright poisonous (log loss 0.740), because the
member who reliably supported procedural motions as a majority member
reliably opposes them in the minority. A synthetic two-world experiment
in the appendix isolates the mechanism: where behavior is
majority-role-driven, member history anti-transfers exactly as
observed; where behavior is a stable trait, it transfers nearly
perfectly. Real congressional voting is a blend, and on the procedural
votes that fill the calendar, the role component dominates.

Because one transition proves little, we repeat the exercise at every
House transition in the text-era window---the 110th, 112th, 116th, and
118th Congresses, where the majority flipped, against the 113th,
115th, 117th, and 119th as placebo transitions
(Figure~\ref{fig:transitions}). The design separates two costs.
Crossing \emph{any} congress boundary is expensive---congress-keyed
member histories collapse everywhere---but the flip-specific damage is
located precisely where the role mechanism predicts: the champion's
procedural-vote log loss averages \flipProcLL{} across flips against
\placeboProcLL{} across placebos, while final-passage transfer is
essentially flip-invariant (\flipPassLL{} versus \placeboPassLL).
Passage coalitions travel; procedural coalitions are creatures of who
holds the gavel. The general claim is accordingly narrowed to what the
evidence shows: what fails to survive a change in majority control is
the \emph{procedural-role} component of voting behavior---which is,
however, a large share of the modern rollcall agenda.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=.98\linewidth]{figures/transitions.pdf}
\caption{The transfer wall at every transition. Left: champion test
log loss for each House test congress, trained only on prior
congresses (red bars: majority flipped; dashes: the majority-status
count table). Right: the same transfer by vote type---the flip damage
concentrates in procedural votes; final-passage transfer barely
notices who won the election.}
\label{fig:transitions}
\end{figure} Attempts to
repair the model with majority-status features fail for an
identifiable reason---within a congress, member history is strictly
more informative, so the model never learns to use the features that
would survive the flip. Meanwhile the logit on frozen, career-spanning
DW-NOMINATE scores nearly matches the deep model's transfer accuracy
(\nomctxCongressoutAcc\% versus \champCongressoutAcc\%).

For measurement practice the implications are direct, and they extend
a caution the American political development literature has pressed on
other grounds: cross-time comparisons of vote-based scores are
entangled with changes in what the chamber votes on
\citep{bateman2017house}. Vote-derived scores estimated within one
majority configuration partly measure that configuration; comparisons
of member scores across a change in control confound preference change
with role change; and the durability of
career-spanning estimation---long derided as an assumption of
implausible preference stability---looks instead like insurance against
role contamination. For the benchmark, cross-configuration transfer is
the posted challenge: no model we tested, and none we could design
under pre-registration, solves it.

\section{Conclusion}

Better prediction has made Congress more measurable: positions that
match the classical canon, loyalty separated from ideology, issue
positions recovered from votes alone, an agenda mapped bill by bill,
and a systematic catalog of the votes no model saw coming. It has also
made the limits measurable, and they are not incidental: legislative
language organizes some coalitions and not others, and a large share of
observed voting behavior belongs to institutional role rather than to
the member. Instruments, measures, and their documented boundaries are
released together---including a frozen model whose forecasting claims
the future itself will continue to audit.

\section{The Interactive Forecaster}
\label{app:dashboard}

The dashboard wraps the frozen champion's MiniLM component tower. (An
earlier draft claimed the blend's TF--IDF component \emph{could not}
run on a single pasted bill; that was wrong---frozen idf weights
transform new documents fine. The component is omitted as an
engineering simplification, justified by its small blend weight of
0.12.)
Pasted text is assembled into the training text template and embedded
with the training encoder; a synthetic bill record carries the
user-specified sponsoring party through the same features used in
training; member vote probabilities are computed for the full current
roster; and the displayed cutpoint is the position at which predicted
support crosses one half, from a logistic fit of predicted votes on the
current congress's estimated positions---mirroring the paper's
cutpoint estimation on realized votes. The display rule guards both
failure modes of that fit: a cutpoint is reported only when the fitted
slope exceeds a minimum magnitude \emph{and} the crossing falls within
the observed range of current-member positions; otherwise it is
suppressed as unidentified, since a logistic fitted to near-unanimous
predictions can cross one half far outside the chamber. Sanity
behavior: a commemorative
post-office naming yields near-unanimous predicted support and no
identified cutpoint; a sponsor-aligned tax bill yields a party-line
forecast with a center-left cutpoint. Off-agenda hypotheticals trigger
the extrapolation flag described in the text. The service is a single
CPU process suitable for a small container host; deployment
configuration accompanies the repository.

\section{The Experiment Ledger}
\label{app:ledger}

Every development experiment was pre-registered in the repository's
ledger before its first run, with a hypothesis, a pre-vote-knowability
argument for any new feature, and a falsification test. Selection used
validation sets only; the three-model final comparison observed the
test sets once. The ledger records, with diagnosed mechanisms: the
encoder-scale negative (a $16\times$ larger text budget does not
improve forecasts and worsens long-bill votes); the amendment-text
negatives and their placebo control (Section~\ref{sec:limits}); the
recency-weighting negative (decayed member rates add estimator noise
exceeding any drift signal); the within-bill context negative (features
that pass synthetic tests at the information-theoretic optimum
destabilize joint training on real data); the per-bucket calibration
negative; and the regime-inheritance result (parameters fitted only on the
temporal development slice---inside training data---transfer to
temporal evaluation and break on interleaved evaluation). Full
reproduction instructions accompany the repository, and
Table~\ref{tab:ledger} gives the compact audit trail for the
experiments this paper relies on most.
